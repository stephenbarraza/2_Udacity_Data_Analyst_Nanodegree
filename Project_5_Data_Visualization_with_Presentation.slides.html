Data Visualization of Prosper Loan Data

The Question:¶

Through four examples, can we explain the variation between Interest Rate and APR using a third variable?¶

What do we know at the start of this project? Most of have taken out a loan at some point. So most of us know that in the loan funding process, a customer's interest rate is established prior to an APR.

The interest rate (often called Rate) mostly consists of market conditions around the prime interest rate as well as a customer's creditworthiness.

We also know that APR is determined by the bank adding fees (loan orgination, assessment, document fees, etc.,) to the cost of the loan. These fees are added up and converted into percentages points. The percentage points are added onto the (previously determined) interest rate and the sum of those becomes the APR.

If, the fees that determine the APR are constant, meaning they are applied uniformly throughout the range of interest rates, then the relationship of Interest Rate to APR should be quite linear (uniform), with very little variation.

That's what we'll be exploring here. How close to linear is the relationship between Interest Rate to APR?

And, if variation is present, can an additioanal third variable be introduced (like debt to income ration, credit score, loan term and loan orgination date) to see if that explains some of that variation?

Let's create a "Perfect World" example of Interest Rate to APR.¶

We can illustrate the linear relationship between interest rate & APR by creating a perfect enviroment showcasing just that.

We'll create an Interest Rate column ("Rate") and a NoFees column (where an APR equals the Interest Rate) and a WithFees column (where any point on the Interest Rate would add an additional 0.005 respresenting lender fees to arrive at an APR).

Illustrating that table graphically, our "perfect world" example of Interest Rate & APR graphically looks like this:

Notice two important points from the previous slide:

First, perfect linearity is shown in both graphs because fees were evenly distributed throughout the range of interst rates.

Second, there was a slight vertical, y-axis (APR) shift (0.005) when fees were applied to any given interest rate.

These two points (perfect linearity & a slight shift) are important to bear in mind as we leave the "perfect world" and enter the "real world" of Prosper Loans.

A better test to visually compare how closely our real world enviroment mirrors our perfect world enviornment is through a scatterplot which graphs the bivariate relationship between two variables like Rate & APR.

Prior to that however, we'll begin by calculating a correlation coefficient.

A correlation coefficient is number that ranges between -1.0 and 1.0. Its purpose is to show the strength of a relationship between two variables. The option varies from a very strong, negative relationship, through no relationship, to a very strong positive relationship. Here, positive and negative represent nothing more than the direction of the relationship when graphed on an x-y axis.

After running the calculations, our correlation coefficient is: 0.9898239703033139

This indicates a very strong positive relationship of BorrowerRate to BorrowerAPR.

In this case, the correlation coefficient has indicated a very strong positive relationship of BorrowerRate to BorrowerAPR.

The following scatterplot of Rate to APR should comfirm the previous slide's findings. We should look for a very strong, linear positive relationship. The advantage here is that scatterplot will visually describe the linearity as well as the variation:

In Conclusion¶

Though these four variables may not have conclusively shown to be potential causes in the variability that lies between Interest Rate & APR, the analyst still very much in the validity of the question being posed: there must be some reason, or multiple reasons, that causes the variability indicated by the initial Rate to APR scatterplot. Further, understanding the parameter of Interest Rate & APR themselves, there really shouldn't be variability at all; all APR fees should be applied uniformally throughout the range of interest rates.

If we were to continue with this investigation, we have a couple of options. First, we could take some of the 3rd variables that showed promise (Prosper Score & Term, perhaps) and investigate them more deeply. Or, we could apply the same format we used here and begin investigating any one or more of the remaining 77 variables that Prosper Loans makes available to us. Whichever path is chosen, the analyst feels it's worth the effort; the answer is out there.
